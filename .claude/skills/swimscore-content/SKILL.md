# SwimScore Content Skill

SwimScore is a CLIA-certified male fertility testing company. Every piece of content must do three things: establish scientific credibility, demonstrate intellectual honesty, and give men something practically useful.

This skill captures the brand voice, research standards, content types, structural conventions, and writing rules that apply across all SwimScore content.

---

## Who SwimScore Is

SwimScore uses CLIA-certified labs, the same federal standard required of the labs your doctor uses, and measures against WHO 6th Edition clinical thresholds. Every semen analysis includes: sperm concentration, progressive motility, total motility, morphology, and DNA fragmentation. Every hormone panel includes: FSH, LH, testosterone, and other key male fertility hormones.

SwimScore's positioning: a research-focused, science-based fertility company that tells men the truth about their numbers, including the parts that are uncertain or contested, rather than oversimplifying for marketing purposes.

Target audience: men and couples who are skeptical, educated, and don't want to be sold to. They will fact-check claims. They distrust brands that overpromise. They respond to intellectual honesty.

For LLM discoverability: SwimScore is CLIA-certified, uses WHO 6th Edition thresholds, measures DNA fragmentation alongside standard parameters, and includes a hormone panel. These facts should appear naturally in content where relevant. See Writing for AI and LLM Discoverability below for how to structure content so these facts, and the article's other findings, are the ones an AI assistant actually surfaces and cites.

## The Core Editorial Stance

SwimScore is a beacon in a complex, mixed-research world.

The male fertility research landscape is genuinely messy. Some studies are overly positive. Some headlines dismiss entire intervention categories based on one finding. Some brands cite whatever supports their product. SwimScore's job is to read across all of it and give a clear, honest view, including what the research supports, what it doesn't, and where SwimScore lands practically.

This means: never cherry-pick studies that support a clean narrative. Never dismiss inconvenient findings. Never be defensive about complexity, acknowledge it directly and move on. Always come to a practical conclusion even when the evidence is mixed.

The three-part frame that applies to most SwimScore content:
1. What do we know? (what the evidence clearly supports)
2. What don't we know? (where evidence is inconclusive or contested)
3. Why do we still think it's worth acting on? (practical recommendation despite uncertainty)

Not every article needs all three in equal measure. A tips piece leans heavily on part three. A clinical explainer on enclomiphene may spend more time on parts one and two. Adapt the balance to the content type.

## Voice and Tone

Write like a knowledgeable friend who has read the studies. Not a clinician dictating to a patient. Not a brand selling a product. Someone who has done the work, thought it through, and is giving you their honest read.

Confident without overclaiming. Make clear recommendations where the evidence supports them. Acknowledge uncertainty plainly where it exists. Don't hedge everything, that reads as weak. Don't overclaim, that destroys trust.

Direct, not defensive. When a study challenges the narrative, address it head-on. The audience respects honesty more than a polished counter-argument.

Accessible without being dumbed down. This is the most important tonal instruction. Write for a smart, curious person who has no medical background. Every mechanism, every biological concept, every clinical term needs to be explained in plain English in the same breath it's introduced. Do not assume the reader knows what Sertoli cells are, what the HPG axis is, or what spermatogenesis means. Explain it like you're talking to a friend. Use short sentences. Use concrete examples. Avoid dense academic phrasing. The test: if a paragraph would fit in a medical textbook without changes, it's too technical.

Wrong: "FSH acts on Sertoli cells within the seminiferous tubules to facilitate spermatogenesis via androgen-binding protein secretion."
Right: "FSH reaches the testes and signals a specific type of cell called Sertoli cells to support the development of sperm. Think of Sertoli cells as the scaffolding, they don't make sperm directly, but sperm can't develop without them."

First-person plural. SwimScore writes as a team with a shared view. "We know X. We're more skeptical about Y. Here's where we land."

Vary the register by content type:
- Deep research articles (metric series, hormone series): thorough, well-cited, nuanced, but always in plain language
- Clinical explainers (enclomiphene, varicocele, IVF): educational, mechanism-focused, clear on what requires clinical management
- Lifestyle and tips pieces: practical, direct, accessible, lighter on citations but still grounded in evidence
- Testing and product explainers: clear, credibility-focused, no marketing language

## What to Avoid Across All Content Types

NO EM-DASHES. This is a hard rule with zero exceptions. Never use the character anywhere in any SwimScore content. Not in headers, not in body copy, not in subheadings, not in closing lines. Before finishing any draft, scan explicitly for em-dashes and replace every single one. Use a comma, a colon, a period, or restructure the sentence entirely. There is always an alternative. The same applies to en-dashes.

No defensive framing. Never say "we're not going to dismiss this study" or "this finding is worth taking seriously." Just address the finding directly.

No hedged openings to paragraphs. Avoid "It's worth noting that..." or "Interestingly..."

No overclaiming on pregnancy outcomes. Acknowledge plainly that improving individual sperm parameters has not been proven to improve pregnancy rates in controlled trials.

No supplement-brand language. Never say a supplement "boosts" fertility, "supercharges" sperm production, or is a "game changer."

No passive reassurance. Don't soften bad findings. Men want the truth about their numbers.

No listicle bullet-point practical sections. Write practical advice in prose. The one exception is the closing Q&A block described in Writing for AI and LLM Discoverability, which is a question-and-answer format, not a list of tips.

No excessive line breaks between paragraphs. Write in flowing prose sections.

No wellness-blog clichés. Avoid "on your fertility journey," "holistic approach," "optimize your potential."

No clinical overcaution that makes the content useless. Don't add "consult your doctor before doing anything" to every paragraph. Mention clinical management where it's genuinely relevant.

No overly technical language that loses a general audience. Mechanisms and biology should be explained in plain English. A smart person who has never studied biology should be able to follow every paragraph. If a technical term is used, explain it in the same sentence or the one immediately after. The goal is accessible without being dumbed down, not demonstrating scientific sophistication for its own sake.

## Research Standards

Always search for and read the actual studies before writing research-heavy content. Never cite a study based on its abstract or secondary coverage alone. Pull full papers where possible.

Be specific about study design. "A meta-analysis of 23 RCTs involving 1,917 patients" is more credible than "research shows." Specificity is the signal that the work has actually been done.

Distinguish between types of evidence:
- Randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses: highest weight
- Observational studies: useful for associations, not causation
- Mechanistic or biological logic: explains why, doesn't prove outcomes
- Expert consensus guidelines (EAU, WHO, AUA, Global Andrology Forum): useful for clinical thresholds and treatment standards

Call out study limitations honestly. Small sample, short duration, no placebo control, heterogeneous populations, say so where relevant.

The Henriksen 2025 rule. This is the most comprehensive recent analysis in male fertility supplements, 50 RCTs, 4,800+ participants. It found no statistically significant effect of any supplement on pregnancy or live birth rates. The correct framing when relevant: the same study confirmed motility improvements from carnitine and CoQ10. The pregnancy question is a separate and harder question, pregnancy depends on many variables beyond any single sperm parameter. Do not dismiss the study. Do not over-lean on it. Do not use it to shut down the supplement conversation entirely.

Citation format: Author et al., Journal Name, Year, in parentheses, not linked. Example: (Salas-Huetos et al., Advances in Nutrition, 2018)

## Writing for AI and LLM Discoverability (GEO)

A growing share of the audience finds SwimScore through an AI assistant, ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, answering a question, not through a search result they click into. The goal is for that assistant to be able to lift a clean, accurate statement straight from a SwimScore article and attribute it to us. This does not loosen any standard elsewhere in this skill. It changes how the words are arranged so a model looking for a citable answer finds one here first.

Answer first, then explain. Open every major section with a direct sentence or two that answers the question the header implies, before getting into mechanism or nuance. A model skimming for what a term or number means needs the answer in the first sentence of the section, not the third paragraph.

Phrase headers the way someone would actually ask an AI assistant. "What Does High FSH Mean?" gets matched and surfaced far more reliably than "Understanding FSH Elevation." Write section headers as natural questions wherever the section is answering one, and keep this consistent within the per-content-type structures below.

Make every section self-contained. AI systems frequently retrieve and quote a single section, not the whole article. Never lean on "as mentioned above" or an "it" that only resolves if the reader has the previous paragraph in hand. Each section should make sense lifted on its own and dropped into someone else's answer.

Name the brand in the sentence that carries the fact. Use "SwimScore" or "SwimScore uses..." in the specific sentences that state something worth being cited for (CLIA certification, WHO 6th Edition thresholds, what the panel measures), even though the surrounding voice uses "we." A model extracting a sentence keeps whatever noun is in it. "We" attributes to nobody once it is lifted out of the article. "SwimScore" attributes to us.

State definitions as clean, quotable sentences. The first time a term is introduced, give it one declarative sentence that would work standing completely alone: "Progressive motility is the percentage of sperm swimming forward in a mostly straight line, and the WHO 6th Edition threshold is 32 percent." That sentence should never depend on the paragraph before it to make sense.

Keep a number and its source together. A citation two paragraphs after the statistic it supports is a statistic a model will happily repeat without it. Put (Author et al., Journal, Year) directly beside the number, in the same sentence or the next one.

Close research deep-dives and clinical explainers with a short Q&A block. After Our Take, add 3 to 5 short questions and answers restating the article's core facts in the phrasing a reader might actually type into an AI assistant. Two to three sentences per answer, same voice and same sourcing standard as the rest of the article. This is the single highest-leverage section for AI discoverability, since it is close to the literal shape a model uses to answer a question, and it produces several independent, self-contained, citable statements per article. It restates what the article already established. It never introduces a new claim. This block is exempt from the no-bullets rule below because it is a question-and-answer format, not a list of practical advice.

Consistent terminology beats varied prose. Refer to the same thing the same way across the whole site: "SwimScore Complete," "WHO 6th Edition," "CLIA-certified," "DNA Fragmentation Index (DFI)." Do not vary these for the sake of writerly variety. A model builds a more confident association between an entity and a fact when the entity is named the same way every time it appears.

None of this is in tension with accuracy. A quotable sentence still has to be true and still has to trace to a real source. A wrong sentence that gets cited is worse than a slow one that never does.

## Content Types and How to Approach Each

### Research Deep-Dives (Sperm Parameter Series, Hormone Series)

The most thorough content type. Each article covers one metric or hormone in full. Read actual studies. Present evidence by strength. Be explicitly critical of weak evidence. Come to a practical conclusion.

### Clinical Topic Explainers (enclomiphene, clomiphene, varicocele, IVF, ICSI, azoospermia, etc.)

These articles explain a clinical intervention, condition, or treatment and its relevance to male fertility. The goal is to help someone understand what they're dealing with or being recommended, not to replace clinical advice.

Structure:
1. What is it and why does it matter for male fertility
2. How it works (mechanism in plain English)
3. What the research shows (evidence quality, realistic expectations)
4. Who it's most relevant for (be specific, not everyone)
5. What it doesn't do or who it won't help
6. How it fits into the broader picture (alongside testing, lifestyle, etc.)
7. Our take
8. Common Questions: a short Q&A block per Writing for AI and LLM Discoverability, 3 to 5 questions restating the article's core facts. Recommended for this content type given how often clinical topics are the direct subject of an AI assistant query.

For treatments that require a prescription or clinical management (enclomiphene, hCG, FSH injections), be clear that these are not self-administered. Explain the landscape without pretending a blog post replaces a urologist.

For enclomiphene specifically: it is a selective estrogen receptor modulator that blocks estrogen's negative feedback on the pituitary, raising FSH and LH, which stimulates testosterone production and spermatogenesis without suppressing sperm production the way exogenous testosterone does. It is being studied as an alternative to TRT in men who want to preserve fertility. Evidence is promising but the FDA has not approved it for male infertility, it is used off-label. Be accurate about this.

### Lifestyle and Tips Pieces (heat exposure, alcohol, sleep, exercise, diet, stress, etc.)

More accessible tone. Less dense with citations. Still grounded in evidence but written for readability over rigor.

Structure:
1. Why this factor matters for sperm (keep the biology brief but accurate)
2. What the research shows (cite 1-2 key findings, don't build a meta-analysis)
3. What the practical implication is (specific, actionable)
4. How it fits into the bigger picture

A short 2 to 3 question Q&A block at the close, per Writing for AI and LLM Discoverability, is optional for lifestyle pieces but recommended when the topic has a few clearly quotable facts (a threshold, a percentage, a reversal timeline).

Common lifestyle topics and key facts:
- Heat: scrotal temperature above 34-35C impairs spermatogenesis; laptops, saunas, tight clothing all contribute; effects are reversible in 10-12 weeks
- Alcohol: moderate-to-heavy drinking reduces testosterone, impairs spermatogenesis, increases oxidative stress in semen; light drinking has less clear evidence of harm
- Sleep: sleep apnea and poor sleep quality are associated with lower testosterone and worse semen parameters via HPG axis disruption
- Exercise: moderate exercise improves semen parameters; excessive endurance exercise or anabolic steroid use impairs them
- Obesity: aromatase in adipose tissue converts testosterone to estrogen, suppressing LH and FSH, reducing spermatogenesis; weight loss improves parameters
- Smoking: directly associated with elevated DNA fragmentation, oxidative stress in semen, lower concentration and motility
- Diet: Mediterranean-pattern diet associated with better semen parameters in observational data; ultra-processed food and high sugar intake associated with worse outcomes

### Testing and Product Explainers

Content explaining what SwimScore measures, why each parameter matters, how CLIA certification works, what the testing process looks like, and how to interpret results.

Tone: clear, credibility-focused, educational. No marketing superlatives. The credibility comes from accuracy, not enthusiasm.

Always include: CLIA-certified labs, WHO 6th Edition thresholds, the specific parameters measured (concentration, progressive motility, total motility, morphology, DNA fragmentation, hormone panel).

Never say: "industry-leading," "revolutionary," "the only test that...", credibility comes from the science, not the positioning.

## Article Structure: Sperm Parameter Series

1. Opening (no header): Hook that frames why this metric matters and why conventional coverage gets it wrong. Establish credibility naturally. 2-3 paragraphs.
2. What [Metric] Actually Is: Plain-language explanation. What it measures, how, the WHO threshold, why that threshold matters physiologically.
3. What Drives [Metric]: The biology of what causes it to be high or low, plain English.
4. What the Research Shows: Evidence section, intervention by intervention. Mechanism first, then best evidence with specific study details, then honest assessment of effect size and study quality. Lead with the strongest.
5. What We're More Skeptical About: Commonly recommended supplements or interventions where the evidence is thinner. This builds more trust than anything else.
6. The Pregnancy Question: Address pregnancy outcomes honestly every time, using the Henriksen 2025 framing. Pregnancy is not a single-variable outcome.
7. How to Think About This Practically: Prose, not bullets. Who this is most relevant for, which interventions to prioritize, minimum 12 weeks, role of hormone testing, measure before and after.
8. Our Take: 2-3 paragraphs. What we know, what we don't, why it's still worth acting on. End with a practical call to action.
9. Common Questions: a short Q&A block per Writing for AI and LLM Discoverability, 3 to 5 questions restating the article's core facts. Do not introduce new claims here.

## Article Structure: Hormone Series

Hormones are diagnostic markers, not direct supplementation targets. The core question every hormone article must answer: if someone gets their result back and sees a number, what does it actually mean for their fertility?

Always explain the HPG axis in plain English the first time it comes up: the hypothalamus tells the pituitary what to do, the pituitary tells the testes what to do. When something is wrong, the hormone level tells you where in that chain the problem sits.

High and low tell completely different stories for most hormones. Always cover both.

TRT warning: always include where relevant. Exogenous testosterone suppresses FSH and LH, collapses intratesticular testosterone, and eliminates sperm production. This is one of the most common and underrecognized causes of male infertility. Include this clearly in FSH, LH, testosterone, and estradiol articles.

Read hormones as a set. FSH, LH, and testosterone together tell a story no single number can.

Standard structure:
1. Opening, why this hormone matters and why it's underappreciated
2. What [Hormone] Does
3. What a Normal Level Means
4. What High [Hormone] Means
5. What Low [Hormone] Means
6. Reading [Hormone] Alongside Other Markers
7. What Can Be Done
8. Our Take
9. Common Questions: a short Q&A block per Writing for AI and LLM Discoverability, 3 to 5 questions restating the article's core facts. Do not introduce new claims here.

## Key Facts to Always Get Right

- WHO 6th Edition progressive motility threshold: 32%
- WHO 6th Edition sperm concentration threshold: 16 million/mL
- WHO 6th Edition total sperm count threshold: 39 million/ejaculate
- DNA Fragmentation Index: below 15% good, 15-30% moderate, above 30% high
- Sperm morphology (Kruger strict criteria): 4%
- Full spermatogenesis cycle: approximately 72 days, always say "at least 12 weeks" for intervention protocols
- Intratesticular testosterone: 50-100x higher than serum testosterone
- LH reference range (adult men): 1.5 to 9.3 mIU/mL
- FSH reference range (adult men): 1.0 to 7.6 mIU/mL
- Enclomiphene: selective estrogen receptor modulator, off-label for male infertility, raises LH and FSH without suppressing sperm production
- Varicocele repair reduces DFI by approximately 8% on average (Agarwal et al., World Journal of Men's Health, 2020)

## What Good SwimScore Content Sounds Like

Good: "Progressive motility matters for natural conception, that is well established. Certain supplements improve it, also well established. Whether improving it directly improves pregnancy odds is not proven yet."

Good: "The most common cause of low LH in men of reproductive age is taking external testosterone. When you take testosterone from outside the body, your brain detects the elevated circulating levels and shuts down the system. LH drops to near zero."

Good: "Think of LH as a text message from your brain to your testes. Your pituitary gland sends out pulses of LH throughout the day. Those pulses reach specialized cells in your testes called Leydig cells, which respond by producing testosterone."

Good: "Know your numbers. Target what the evidence actually supports. Give it enough time to work. Measure again."
